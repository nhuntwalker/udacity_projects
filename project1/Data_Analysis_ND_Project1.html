Question 1: What is our independent variable? What is our dependent variable?

  • Our independent variable is the color of each word.
  • The dependent variable is then the amount of time taken to name the colors in the list.

Question 2: What is an appropriate set of hypotheses for this task? What kind of statistical test do you expect to perform? Justify your choices.

  • An appropriate null hypothesis $H_0$ is that on average, there is no significant increase in reading time between congruent and incongruent lists. Numerically, this would be $\mu_C \ge \mu_I$.
  • An alternate hypothesis $H$ is then that on average the incongruent lists take significantly longer to read than the congruent lists. Numerically, this would be $\mu_C < \mu_I$.
  • From information about the initial study, as well as trying the experiment myself, it seems that the incongruent list takes longer for the brain to process. So, to test this, I should assume that there is no significant time lag, and accept significant ($\alpha < 0.05$) negative deviations (congruent time < incongruent time) from $H_0$ as a rejection of $H_0$.
  • To test $H$ I intend to do a left-tailed t-test for 2 dependent samples. I'm choosing this test instead of the independent samples t-test since the same person is being tested twice in a row on two slightly different but ultimately similar stimuli (repeated measures design).
  • I'm choosing the t-test in lieu of a z-test because these don't represent full populations, the sample size is small (< 30), and we don't have access to descriptive statistics of the full populations.
  • I'm also choosing the t-test instead of other types of tests for non-gaussian distributions because there's nothing in the data (or in the hypothesis) to suggest that these data are anything but normally distributed.

def t_statistics(df, alpha=0.05):
    mu1 = df.Congruent.mean()
    n1 = len(df.Congruent)
    
    mu2 = df.Incongruent.mean()
    n2 = len(df.Incongruent)

    d = df.Congruent - df.Incongruent
    s_d = np.std(d, ddof=1)
    
    se = s_d / np.sqrt(len(d))
    t = (mu1 - mu2)/se
    
    pvals = np.array([0.25, 0.2, .15, .1, .05, .025, .02, .01, .005, .0025, .001, .0005])
    tcrits = -np.array([0.685, 0.858, 1.060, 1.319, 1.714, 2.069, 2.177, 2.500, 2.807, 3.104, 3.485, 3.768])
    
    return t, pvals[tcrits > t][-1]

def confidence_interval(indata, CI=0.95):
    mu = indata.mean()
    std_err = np.std(indata, ddof=1)/np.sqrt(len(indata))
    
    pvals = np.array([0.25, 0.2, .15, .1, .05, .025, .02, .01, .005, .0025, .001, .0005])
    conf_ints = np.array([0.5, 0.6, 0.7, 0.8, 0.9, 0.95, 0.96, 0.98, 0.99, 0.995, 0.998, 0.999])
    
    tcrits = np.array([0.685, 0.858, 1.060, 1.319, 1.714, 2.069, 2.177, 2.500, 2.807, 3.104, 3.485, 3.768])
    tcrit = tcrits[conf_ints == CI]
    
    min_t = float(mu - tcrit * std_err)
    max_t = float(mu + tcrit * std_err)
    
    return min_t, max_t

My calculated $t$-statistic is roughly -8.021. For my criteria of $\alpha <$ 0.05, the critical value was -1.714. In fact, my $t$-statistic is far below even the lowest $\alpha$ of 0.0005 ($t_\text{crit}$ of -3.768).

With this in mind, I'm definitely ready to reject the null hypothesis. There is definitely a longer reading time for the incongruent data set. We can see this if we look at the confidence intervals for reading times:

The results absolutely matched up with my expectations. When practicing the experiment on my own, it was noticeably harder to process the list when the colors didn't match up. I had to spend more conscious time thinking about each word than the congruent list, where after a while I didn't even need to process the colors because I noticed a trend.

Question 6 (optional): What do you think is responsible for the effects observed? Can you think of an alternative or similar task that would result in a similar effect? Some research about the problem will be helfpul for thinking about these two questions!

I've previously mentioned what I think is responsible for the observed effects, but I'll restate here: there's more conscious effort in processing the incongruent word list, whereas when I'm seeing that the words match up to their colors I'm going to expect the next word to follow that trend and process the list faster. When I'm going through the incongruent list and making errors, I'm going to slow down to make sure I get it right.

A variation on this experiment might be mixing directional words with their positions on a page. For example, the set of compass directions (N, NE, E, SE, S, SW, W, NW) being paired with their positions on a compass, then being mixed up with those same positions.

Thank you for extending and thinking deeper with the investigation. Do you think that the effects of incongruency can be observed in other senses like hearing and touch?

I would imagine that you could see the effects of incongruency by mixing the associations of a thing with the things themselves. It's easiest to see in language because it's based entirely on associations with objects and experiences. Showing a computer and calling it a chair is going to disrupt a well-established brain pathway and take longer to recognize. Similarly, seeing a dog go "moo" or biting into a wax apple would produce the same difficulty in recognition.
